## 2. PUBLISHED UNIVERSE & OUR MAPPING

- **What the sources trade:** Both books are instrument-generic. McMillan's Ch. 9 examples are single listed US stocks ("XYZ") with the old quarterly expiration cycle (April/July/October). Natenberg's Ch. 8 examples span futures options (Eurodollar, currencies) and stock options (IBM named as example, p. 153ff). Neither book restricts calendars to an index.
- **Our mapping:** SPY / QQQ / IWM plus the liquid mega-cap tier AAPL / NVDA / MSFT / TSLA / AMD / META. Monthly (3rd-Friday) expirations only, to stay closest to the sources' monthly-cycle structure.
- **Why the mapping preserves the edge claim:** The published edge is structural, not name-specific: (a) ATM near-term time value decays faster than ATM far-term time value (McMillan p. 191 states the concept - time "will erode the value of the near-term option at a faster rate than it will the far-term option"; the verbatim quote "attempting to capitalize on the known fact that the near-term call will lose time premium more rapidly" is his p. 199 Summary wording); (b) the spread is net long vega, so buying it when implied volatility is cheap adds positive expected edge (Natenberg pp. 166–167). Both mechanisms operate identically on ETF and mega-cap options; our tier has the tightest spreads and deepest OI in the US market, which *reduces* the commission/slippage drag both books repeatedly warn dominates this strategy.
- **ADAPTATION - none required structurally.** The published form is already options-only, two legs, no stock leg, no continuous delta-hedging. Natenberg notes time spreads "will be ratioed delta neutral" for his analysis (1994 p. 149) but states they are "most commonly executed one-to-one" (p. 149); we implement the common 1:1 form, which is also McMillan's form. ETF/mega-cap short calls carry ex-dividend early-assignment risk (SPY/IWM quarterly ex-div; AAPL/MSFT ex-div) - handled by McMillan's own parity rule (section 4).

## 3. EXACT ENTRY RULES

Every constant here carries a quote or precise locator. Where the books publish NO number, it says UNKNOWN.

1. **Structure.** Sell one nearer-expiry option, buy one farther-expiry option, same strike, same type, 1:1. McMillan 5th ed. p. 191: a calendar spread "involves the sale of one option and the simultaneous purchase of a more distant option," both at the same striking price. Natenberg 1994 p. 149: time spreads are "most commonly executed one-to-one".
2. **Type = calls.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 334: "Neutral call calendar spreads are generally superior to neutral put calendar spreads" (rationale: calls usually carry more time premium unless the stock pays a large dividend).
3. **Strike = ATM.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 192: the neutral spread is the one "established when the underlying stock is at or near the striking price"; Natenberg's analysis assumes the options are "approximately at-the-money" (1994 p. 149). Implementation: listed strike nearest to spot at entry.
4. **Front-leg DTE = 8–12 weeks.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 194: "It is generally best to establish such a spread 8 to 12 weeks before the near-term option expires," because under 8 weeks the near-term option's decay rate "increases substantially" relative to longer options. This is the only hard DTE number published in either primary source.
5. **Back leg = intermediate-term.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 199 (Summary): "the most common approach is to sell the nearest-term call and buy the intermediate-term call." In his quarterly-cycle examples that is a 3-month gap (April 50 / July 50, p. 191). No modern-calendar DTE number is published; see parameter table for our ADAPTED default. (Secondary: Options Playbook builds it "with expiration one month later" - a documented variant, not the McMillan default.)
6. **IV entry gate - the "low IV" condition.** Natenberg 1994 pp. 166–167 (Choosing an Appropriate Strategy): "If options generally appear underpriced (low implied volatility), look for spreads with a positive vega. This includes strategies in the backspread or long time spread category." And p. 152: "When implied volatility rises, time spreads tend to widen; when implied volatility falls, time spreads tend to narrow." Natenberg defines "low" *relative to the trader's own volatility estimate* (p. 166: comparing "our volatility estimate to the implied volatility in the option marketplace") - he publishes NO percentile/rank threshold. McMillan adds the negative gate, p. 194: "Sometimes, calendar spreads look especially attractive when the underlying stock is volatile. However, this can be misleading" (vol contraction then shrinks the spread). **Published numeric threshold: UNKNOWN. Our operationalization is ADAPTED** (see table: IV-rank < 30, VIX-percentile fallback).
7. **Entry timing (time of day / T-n to events):** UNKNOWN - neither book publishes a time-of-day or event-relative entry rule. (Natenberg pp. 152–153 discusses scheduled events - Fed statements, "a company's earnings are due" - as things that *raise* IV and widen time spreads, but gives no entry-timing rule around them.)
8. **Regime gate beyond IV:** none published. UNKNOWN.

## 4. EXACT EXIT RULES (OVERRIDE platform exit doctrine for this strategy)

1. **Primary exit = time exit at/before front expiry, closing BOTH legs as one spread order.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 192: "In a neutral spread, one should initially have the intent of closing the spread by the time the near-term option expires." Restated p. 334 ("the idea of closing the spread when the near-term call or put expires") and p. 479 ("normally closed when the near-term option expires"). McMillan p. 194: the closing order "should be placed as a single order. 'Legging' out of a spread is highly risky". His Substack article repeats: the spreader should not hold the long option "as a speculation after the near-term option expires."
2. **Assignment guard (the only published pre-expiry hard trigger).** McMillan 5th ed. p. 194: "Prior to expiration, the spreader should close the spread if the near-term short call is trading at parity. He does this to avoid assignment." (Parity = bid ≤ intrinsic value.) On our universe this also covers ex-dividend early-assignment windows for SPY/IWM/mega-caps.
3. **No stop-loss is published; the doctrine on adverse breakouts is explicitly to hold.** McMillan 5th ed. p. 195, on a breakout: of the defensive choices "the easiest and most conservative one is to do nothing at all," letting the short call expire worthless; risk "remains fixed at the original debit." Upside breakout, p. 195: "Doing nothing is often the best course of action." Aggressive defenses (selling the long leg, or covering the short and riding the long) are described and *rejected* for the neutral strategist (pp. 195–196: "extremely aggressive and illogical").
4. **Late-stage profit protection (qualitative, the only other published pre-expiry exit).** McMillan 5th ed. p. 196: with the stock near the strike "with only two weeks to go," the spreader "should be ready to close the position quickly if the stock begins to get too far away from the striking price" - mental stop-out points, no numeric distance published.
5. **Profit target:** UNKNOWN in both primary sources (McMillan p. 192 notes at front expiry "The spread could be closed at this time in order to realize the profit" - that IS the target event). Secondary heuristic only: Options Playbook, "risk one to make two ... For every net debit of $1 at initiation, you're hoping to receive $2 when closing" - recorded as [verified-secondary] aspiration, NOT implemented as a trigger.
6. **Platform tightening (ADAPTED, flagged):** the strategy id mandates exit *before front-expiry week*. McMillan allows holding to front expiration itself; we close the whole spread at T−5 trading days before front expiry (or earlier on rule 2). This tightening is ours: it avoids the front leg's pin/assignment/gamma zone that his parity rule polices manually. Tagged ADAPTED in the table.

## 5. SIZING CONVENTION IN SOURCE

Per-spread debit, paid in full, is the investment and the maximum risk: McMillan 5th ed. p. 191 ("his investment is the net debit of the spread plus commissions") and p. 334 ("must be paid for in full"). No margin beyond the debit for the 1:1 same-strike form. McMillan's only quantity guidance is commission-driven, p. 334: "spreads should be established in a large enough quantity to minimize the percentage cost of commissions ... at least 10 spreads should be set up initially." He also cautions (p. 199, Summary) it stays non-aggressive only if the spreader "does not invest a large percentage of his trading capital" in it. **Our shadow ignores all of this by convention: always 1 published unit (1×1 spread), account-blind.**

## 7. KNOWN FAILURE MODES

- **Large fast move in either direction (the published worst case).** McMillan p. 334: max loss realized "if the underlying stock moved substantially far away from the striking price" by front expiry - both legs converge and the spread "would shrink to practically nothing." Natenberg 1994 p. 152: the long time spreader "expects to lose money if the underlying market makes a swift move in either direction." Historical instances on our universe: Mar-2020 COVID crash, Feb-2018 Volmageddon, Aug-5-2024 vol spike - all are big-realized-move regimes that crush open long calendars even though vega is positive, because movement dominates.
- **IV collapse after entry.** Natenberg 1994 p. 152: falling implied vol narrows the spread and "may more than offset any gain from the passage of time." McMillan p. 194: after a volatile period, if the stock stabilizes, the vol contraction loss "may be greater than the gain from time decay!" This is exactly why entering when IV is already LOW (this strategy's gate) is the published defense.
- **Term-structure inversion in a vol spike (structural, our note - not in the books).** Natenberg's widen-on-IV-rise claim assumes one volatility applied to both months (his Figure 8-14 example raises both from 20% to 25%, 1994 p. 152). In real spikes front-month IV rises far more than back-month; a long calendar (short front vega dollars locally) can lose on the vol event despite net-positive book vega. Tag: platform analysis, [unverified] as to source attribution.
- **Early assignment on the short leg.** McMillan p. 194: assignment risk when the short call trades at parity; "Being called out of spread position is devastating" commission-wise. On our universe the trigger clusters around deep-ITM drift and ex-dividend dates (SPY/IWM quarterly ex-div; AAPL/MSFT/META dividends). Natenberg 1994 pp. 156–157 adds the tender-offer/short-squeeze case where the spread "will collapse to zero" - mega-cap M&A is remote but nonzero (single names only).
- **Dividend increases narrow call time spreads.** Natenberg 1994 p. 155: an increase in dividends "will cause call time spreads to narrow and put time spreads to widen" (his Figure 8-15 table shows the effect). A surprise dividend hike on a mega-cap hurts the call calendar.
- **Earnings gap-through-strike (single names).** Application of the large-move failure mode: an earnings gap inside the front-leg window is the canonical single-stock calendar killer; also IV-crush post-earnings hits the back leg. Our earnings gate (section 9/8) is the defense - PLATFORM-POLICY, since the books publish no earnings-avoidance rule.
- **Commission/slippage drag.** McMillan p. 194: "limited profits and relatively large commission costs" - on a 4-leg round trip (2 in, 2 out), spread-crossing cost is a first-order eater of the edge; mitigated by our penny-wide universe but must be modeled in fills.

## 9. DATA REQUIREMENTS

- **Tradier chains:** monthly expirations in a 40–190 DTE band (front selection 56–84 DTE; back up to front+98d), ATM ±2 strikes is sufficient per symbol; quotes for all 4 fill events plus daily marks on both legs.
- **IV rank inputs:** 30-day ATM IV history per symbol for the 1y rank (row 8). Our IV archive is cold → **VIX 1-year percentile < 30 is the documented fallback gate** until per-symbol rank has ≥1y of history. (For QQQ/IWM, VXN/RVX would be truer but VIX-percentile is the declared fallback.)
- **Earnings calendar:** date + bmo/amc for the 6 single names (row 9 gate); ETFs exempt.
- **Dividend calendar:** ex-div dates for SPY/IWM/QQQ and dividend-paying single names - feeds the parity/assignment guard (row 12) which must be checked daily into ex-div dates. (Natenberg 1994 p. 155 dividend effect.)
- **FOMC/CPI dates:** not gated by source (Natenberg treats scheduled events as IV-raisers, pp. 152–153); not required for entry/exit logic; log-only for attribution.
- **Daily history:** underlying daily closes (strike-distance monitoring, marks); no 1-min bars required - this family needs EOD marks plus an intraday parity check (a once-or-twice-daily short-leg bid vs intrinsic check suffices; see sec. 10).
- **VIX daily series:** for the fallback percentile gate.

## 10. SHADOW-IMPLEMENTATION NOTES

- **Expected trades/month:** ~3 (range 0–6). Basis: 9 symbols, 1 open spread per symbol, holds of ~7–11 weeks (entry 8–12 wks before front expiry, exit T−5d), so a full steady-state book turns over ~1 spread per symbol per ~2.5 months ≈ 3–4 entries/month - but the IV-rank<30 gate is regime-clustered: calm regimes admit most of the universe, stressed regimes admit zero for months. Grading timeline: N≥25 verdicts need roughly 6–9 months of calm-regime operation; plan for slow grading and regime-lumpy samples.
- **Mark cadence:** EOD two-leg marks (mid) are sufficient for P&L; PLUS a daily parity check on the short leg (bid vs intrinsic) that must fire intraday-priority into ex-div dates and whenever the short leg goes ITM. No 1-min stream needed.
- **Multi-leg shape:** 2-leg, same-strike, cross-expiry, same-type (calls), 1:1 - a "calendar" combo type; both entry and exit are single spread orders (McMillan p. 194 no-legging rule applies to fills simulation: fill the spread as a package at combo mid-adjusted price, never leg fills).
- **Capital-at-risk basis:** **debit paid**. This is exact, not a proxy - max loss = net debit (McMillan pp. 192/334). No Reg-T add-on exists for the 1:1 same-strike debit calendar (front expires before back; McMillan p. 334: "paid for in full"). Use debit as the per-trade CaR denominator in the pooled grader.
- **1-lot account-blind distortion:** McMillan's ≥10-lot commission guidance (p. 334) means his net-of-cost arithmetic assumes commission amortization we don't get at 1 lot; our shadow fills must charge full per-spread friction, which biases our result *below* the book's worked examples. Also his "sell a portion of the long side" partial-exit tactics (p. 195) are impossible at 1 lot - we simply don't implement them (they are optional follow-ups, not the core doctrine).
- **Exit-doctrine override note for the platform:** rows 11–13 of the table replace the platform exit ladder for this family - in particular NO stop-loss and NO "number lower now" style exits; the position rides to the time exit or parity guard by published doctrine.
- **Honesty ledger:** the two load-bearing UNKNOWNs are the IV threshold (row 7 - the books give a principle, we chose 30) and the profit target (row 14 - none exists). If a future audit finds a numeric IV threshold or profit target attributed to Natenberg or McMillan in our configs tagged as source-verbatim, that is a falsification - they are ADAPTED/absent respectively.
